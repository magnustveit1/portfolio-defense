Research question

The research question

When Glen Canyon Dam closed in 1963, rising water buried Lower Cataract Canyon's channel under tens of meters of fine sediment, now called the Dominy Formation. Around 2000 a long drought started pulling Lake Powell down, exposing that sediment. Since then buried rapids have reemerged, banks have collapsed, and the river has been cutting back down through the deposit fast.

This project put numbers on that change. Using three airborne LiDAR surveys spanning fifteen years, where did the canyon surface change, by how much, and with what confidence?

All three datasets have vertical accuracy around 10 cm and enough point density for change detection, but they were flown by different programs with different instruments and pixel sizes (1.0 m in 2005 and 2020, 0.5 m in 2015). Datasets like that carry systematic offsets and noise, so I had to measure those before trusting any change estimates.

Methods

Harmonize → difference → characterize error → quantify volume

I first resampled the three DEMs to a matching cell size and datum. Then I computed difference surfaces (DEMs of Difference, or DoDs) with map algebra for three intervals: 2005–2015, 2015–2020, and 2005–2020. Before reading anything into the maps, I checked vertical uncertainty using stable surfaces like bedrock that should not have moved. That gave a noise estimate for each map and caught a systematic +0.2 m offset in the 2005–2020 comparison. Zonal statistics over the study polygons then turned the difference rasters into sediment volumes, following Wheaton et al. (2010).

Results

Results by site

RM 196.7: across from Gypsum Canyon

Between 2015 and 2020 the bank across from the mouth of Gypsum Canyon lost 44,457 m³ from just 2,964 m². That works out to a mean of 15.0 m of erosion in five years, the most intense bank erosion anywhere in the study. The Gypsum Canyon debris fan forms a rapid that pushes flow into the bank, which explains why it concentrates there.

RM 182.8: mouth of Dark Canyon

A bank of only 539 m² lost over 6,300 m³ between 2015 and 2020, a mean of 11.84 m cut into the bank face. It is a small site, but it shows how fast exposed Dominy sediment fails.

RM 177.9: a geomorphic pulse

The same east bank shows up in all three intervals. It lost about 154,850 m³ between 2005 and 2015, then only 2,932 m³ between 2015 and 2020. Most of the change happened within a decade of the sediment being exposed, then it mostly stopped. The 2005–2020 map totals 157,437 m³, and the two interval volumes add up to within 345 m³ (0.2%) of that. The agreement is a useful internal check that the differencing workflow is consistent.

RM 172.7: before Hite Bridge

Just upstream of Hite Bridge, over 270,322 m³ was removed from 31,080 m² of bank between 2005 and 2015, an average loss of 8.7 m. The retreat was steady and large across the whole decade.

Dirty Devil confluence: channel migration

Near the Dirty Devil River, the 2005–2020 comparison shows the channel migrating. The north side gained 128,028 m³ (mean +10.08 m) while the south side lost 104,326 m³ (mean 7.60 m). The river is eroding one bank and building the other. The Garfield/San Juan county line was drawn down the center of the river in the 1880s and now sits south of the channel, so the river has moved north of its pre-dam position.

Riverbed elevation changes

At RM 168 near the Hite Overlook, the riverbed rose by over 1,086,792 m³ across 145,456 m² (mean +7.47 m) between 2005 and 2015. At RM 177 above Sheep Canyon it dropped 206,617 m³ (mean 2.44 m) over the same period. Both signals are broad and uniform, and both are probably affected by how the datasets smooth surfaces classified as water, so I read them more cautiously than the bank results.

Limitations

Limitations

A few limitations matter here. The 2015 DEM is 0.5 m while 2005 and 2020 are 1.0 m, so harmonizing them requires resampling, which probably contributes to the +0.2 m offset in the 2005–2020 comparison. Near-vertical cliffs produce oblique LiDAR returns and elevation errors up to about 20 m in places, so extreme values along cliffs are artifacts, not real change. The 2005 dataset only covers the river corridor, about a third of the study area, which limits both the comparison extent and the stable terrain available for the error check. The datasets smooth surfaces classified as water, so riverbed signals are uncertain, and there are no discharge records for the three flights to help separate water level change from sediment change. Finally, the ground-based photogrammetry models from GEOG 6150 could not be aligned to the DEMs because only two GNSS control points were recorded.
