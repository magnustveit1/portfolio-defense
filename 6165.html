Design rationale

The color scheme

Difference rasters are diverging data. Erosion sits below zero and deposition above, so the color scheme was the biggest design decision. Following Brewer's guidance, the ramp runs from warm red-orange for erosion, through near-white at zero, to cool blue for deposition. It avoids red-green combinations for colorblind readers and uses lightness, not just hue, to show magnitude.

The tricky part was the zero point. The data are not symmetric. Erosion reaches −17 m (RM 196.7) while deposition tops out around +12 m (Dirty Devil confluence). A symmetric range would either clip real erosion or waste color on deposition that never happens. So the display range is −17 to +12 based on the measured maxima, and the white zero stop sits at 59% of the ramp (17 divided by 29) so that white means exactly 0 m.

All three rasters use identical symbology, set up once and copied over with ArcGIS Pro's Import Symbology. The same color means the same amount of change in every time period, so the maps can be compared directly.

The legend was built in ArcGIS Pro's Layout view because AGOL's auto-generated legend can't label an asymmetric diverging ramp correctly. The finished maps and legend are all in the Story Map above.

Narrative design

Writing for a general audience

The Story Map uses Sidecar sections. As the viewer scrolls, each slide pans and zooms the map to the next study site, which keeps people oriented even if they have never seen the canyon. The text assumes no remote sensing or geomorphology background, and technical terms get plain definitions the first time they show up.

Two method notes, one about the rasters covering different extents and one about cliff artifacts, appear right where each issue first becomes visible instead of up front in a methods section. That follows Tufte's idea that annotation should help the viewer at the moment they need it. The layout and legend choices leaned on his data-ink and graphical integrity principles too.
